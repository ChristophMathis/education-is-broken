
# Glossary

| Term | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| 1EdTech | International consortium (formerly IMS Global) that develops open standards for educational technology, including Open Badges and Learning Information Framework (LIF). |
| 2 Sigma Problem | Finding by Benjamin Bloom (1984) that students receiving one-on-one tutoring perform two standard deviations better than students in conventional classrooms — a benchmark for what personalised instruction can achieve. |
| 42 School (École 42) | Tuition-free, peer-to-peer coding school founded in Paris (2013) with no teachers, no lectures, and no formal entry requirements. Uses project-based learning and a selection process called the *piscine*. |
| Adaptive learning | Technology-driven approach that adjusts learning content, pace, and difficulty in real time based on individual learner performance and behaviour. |
| Agency divide | The gap between learners who possess the self-regulation, metacognition, and motivation to direct their own learning and those who do not — a central equity concern in personalised and modular education systems. |
| Agentic AI | AI systems capable of autonomous decision-making and multi-step task execution without continuous human oversight, as distinct from tools that merely assist human decisions. |
| Algorithmic literacy | The capacity to understand how recommendation and decision-making algorithms work, what they optimise for, what they cannot see, and how they can be manipulated. |
| Ausbildung | German term for vocational training or professional preparation — education aimed at qualifying someone for a specific occupation. Contrasted with *Bildung*. |
| AVID | Advancement Via Individual Determination — a US college-readiness programme targeting students in the academic middle, using scaffolded support to increase higher-education completion rates. |
| B2B | Business-to-business — in this context, education providers (e.g. Guild Education) selling learning services to employers rather than directly to individual learners. |
| Batch processing | Metaphor from computing used to describe the traditional university model: fixed cohorts of students processed through standardised sequences on a uniform schedule. |
| Bildung | German concept of education as the holistic development of a person's capacity to think, judge, adapt, and take responsibility — broader than vocational training (*Ausbildung*). |
| Bitkom | Germany's federal association for the digital economy, representing over 2,000 IT, telecommunications, and digital media companies. Frequently cited for ICT labour-market data. |
| Blockcerts | Open standard for creating, issuing, and verifying blockchain-based educational credentials, developed by MIT and Learning Machine. |
| BMBF | Bundesministerium für Bildung und Forschung — the German Federal Ministry of Education and Research, a major funder of research and education policy. |
| Bologna Process | Intergovernmental reform process (launched 1999) to create a unified European Higher Education Area (EHEA) with comparable degrees (Bachelor/Master), credit transfer (ECTS), and quality assurance. |
| Burning Glass Institute | Research organisation analysing labour-market data, job postings, and skills demand; frequently cited on skills-based hiring and credential trends. |
| Career lattice | Alternative to the traditional career ladder — a model where career progression includes lateral, diagonal, and vertical moves across roles and functions. |
| CBE | Competency-Based Education — a model where students advance by demonstrating mastery of defined competencies rather than by accumulating seat-time or credit hours. |
| CDC | Centers for Disease Control and Prevention — US federal public health agency; cited in the book in connection with health-related data and youth well-being. |
| CHARM-EU | Challenge-Driven, Accessible, Research-based, and Mobile European University — a European Universities Initiative alliance piloting challenge-based, transnational degree programmes. |
| Cognitive load | The total amount of mental effort required to process information; a key concept in instructional design (Sweller) that distinguishes intrinsic, extraneous, and germane load. |
| Credential inflation | The progressive devaluation of educational credentials as more people obtain them, requiring ever-higher qualifications for roles that previously demanded less. |
| Credly | Digital credentialing platform that issues and verifies Open Badges and micro-credentials for employers, universities, and professional organisations. |
| CRISPR | Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats — a gene-editing technology; cited as an example of breakthrough research originating in publicly funded university labs. |
| CUNY ASAP | Accelerated Study in Associate Programs at the City University of New York — an intensive support programme (advising, small cohorts, wraparound services) that more than doubled community college graduation rates. |
| Deepfake | Synthetic media generated by AI, typically video or audio that realistically depicts someone saying or doing something they did not — a threat to information integrity. |
| Destatis | Statistisches Bundesamt — Germany's Federal Statistical Office, the primary source for official statistics on education, demographics, and public finance. |
| DFG | Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft — the German Research Foundation, the main self-governing funding body for academic research in Germany. |
| Drittmittel | Third-party funding — research grants and contracts acquired competitively from external sources (DFG, BMBF, EU, industry), as opposed to core institutional funding (*Grundmittel*). |
| ECIU | European Consortium of Innovative Universities — a network of research-intensive universities piloting micro-credentials, challenge-based learning, and flexible degree structures. |
| ECTS | European Credit Transfer and Accumulation System — standardised system for comparing study attainment and performance across European higher education institutions (1 ECTS ≈ 25–30 hours of study). |
| EdTech | Educational technology — the industry and field concerned with using digital tools and platforms to deliver, manage, and assess learning. |
| EHEA | European Higher Education Area — the framework of comparable academic degrees, quality assurance, and credit recognition created through the Bologna Process. |
| Eightfold.ai | AI-powered talent intelligence platform that uses deep learning for skills-based matching, internal mobility, and workforce planning. |
| Emotion AI | AI systems that attempt to detect, interpret, and respond to human emotions through facial expression analysis, voice tone, or physiological signals — controversial in hiring contexts. |
| EQF | European Qualifications Framework — an eight-level reference framework that links national qualification systems across Europe, enabling cross-border comparison of credentials. |
| Erasmus+ | EU programme for education, training, youth, and sport that funds student and staff mobility, institutional partnerships, and policy reform across European countries. |
| EU AI Act | European Union regulation (adopted 2024) classifying AI systems by risk level and imposing obligations for transparency, human oversight, and bias mitigation — particularly strict for AI in hiring and education. |
| EUA | European University Association — the representative organisation of European universities and national rectors' conferences, active in policy advocacy and institutional development. |
| EUI | European Universities Initiative — EU-funded programme supporting transnational alliances of higher education institutions to develop joint programmes, shared governance, and integrated learning pathways. |
| Eurobarometer | Series of public opinion surveys conducted regularly on behalf of the European Commission, covering attitudes on a wide range of topics including education, technology, and employment. |
| Europass wallet | EU digital tool that allows individuals to store, manage, and share verified educational credentials, qualifications, and skills across European countries. |
| Eurostat | Statistical office of the European Union, providing comparable statistics on economy, population, education, and social conditions across EU member states. |
| Eurydice | European Commission network providing information on and analysis of European education systems and policies across 37 countries. |
| Exzellenzstrategie | Germany's Excellence Strategy — a competitive federal-state funding programme that awards clusters of excellence and university status as "Universities of Excellence" based on research performance. |
| Fachhochschulen | German universities of applied sciences — institutions focused on practice-oriented teaching and applied research, as distinct from traditional research universities (*Universitäten*). |
| FCDO | Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office — UK government department responsible for international development and diplomacy; cited as a funder of global education research. |
| Feature drift | Concept from software development: small incremental changes that accumulate unnoticed until the product has become fundamentally different from what was originally designed. Used metaphorically for the book's own evolution. |
| Fuel50 | AI-driven talent marketplace and career-pathing platform that matches employees to internal opportunities, learning, and mentoring based on skills and aspirations. |
| GDPR | General Data Protection Regulation — EU regulation (effective 2018) on data protection and privacy, relevant to AI in hiring and education through requirements on consent, transparency, and data minimisation. |
| Gloat | AI-powered internal talent marketplace platform that matches employees with projects, roles, and learning opportunities within organisations. |
| Grade inflation | The tendency for average grades to rise over time without a corresponding increase in student achievement, undermining the informational value of grades. |
| Grundmittel | Core public funding — the stable, formula-based institutional funding that German universities receive from their respective *Länder* (states) for teaching, basic operations, and infrastructure. Contrasted with *Drittmittel*. |
| Guild Education | US-based B2B education platform that partners with employers to offer tuition-free degree and credential programmes to their workforce, funded by the employer. |
| HEFCE | Higher Education Funding Council for England — the former public body responsible for distributing funding to English universities (replaced by the Office for Students in 2018). |
| HESA | Higher Education Statistics Agency — the UK body responsible for collecting, analysing, and disseminating statistics about higher education. |
| HolonIQ | Market intelligence platform focused on education, providing data, analysis, and benchmarking on global EdTech and higher education trends. |
| Horizon Europe | The EU's key funding programme for research and innovation (2021–2027), with a budget of approximately €95.5 billion. |
| Human-in-the-loop | System design principle requiring meaningful human judgment and oversight at critical decision points in automated or AI-driven processes. |
| Hyperpersonalisation | Advanced form of personalisation in learning or talent management that uses AI, behavioural data, and predictive analytics to tailor content and pathways to individual learners at granular level. |
| ICR | Income-contingent repayment — a student loan repayment model where payments are calculated as a percentage of the borrower's income, reducing financial risk for graduates. |
| Institutional fact | Concept from philosopher John Searle (*The Construction of Social Reality*, 1995): a fact that exists only because of collective human agreement and institutional backing — e.g. a university degree has value because society collectively treats it as meaningful, not because of its physical properties. |
| ICT | Information and Communications Technology — the sector encompassing computing, telecommunications, and digital services; frequently cited in labour-market skills-gap analyses. |
| IDC | International Data Corporation — a global market intelligence firm providing data and analysis on IT, telecommunications, and technology markets. |
| Intelligence factory | Metaphor used in the book for large-scale AI systems that can produce cognitive outputs (analysis, writing, code) at industrial scale, challenging the traditional role of education as knowledge transmission. |
| K-12 | The US designation for primary and secondary education from kindergarten through 12th grade (approximately ages 5–18). |
| Khanmigo | AI-powered tutoring tool developed by Khan Academy using large language models to provide personalised, Socratic-style learning support. |
| Landesmittel | Public funding allocated by individual German federal states (*Länder*) to universities — the primary component of *Grundmittel*. |
| Learning in the Flow of Work | Concept (Josh Bersin) describing learning experiences embedded directly into daily work processes rather than delivered as separate training events. |
| Learning poverty | World Bank metric: the percentage of children who cannot read and understand a simple text by age 10 — used as a baseline indicator of educational system failure. |
| Learning velocity | The rate at which an individual acquires and demonstrates new competencies — an emerging behavioural signal in skills-based hiring and talent analytics. |
| LIF | Learner Information Framework — a set of open standards (developed by 1EdTech) for describing, exchanging, and integrating learner data across systems and institutions. |
| Lifelong learning account | Government-funded or co-funded individual account that citizens can draw on throughout their lives to pay for accredited education and training — portable across employers and institutions. |
| Mastery-based learning | Instructional approach where students must demonstrate thorough understanding of a topic before advancing, rather than progressing on a fixed schedule regardless of comprehension. |
| Matthew effect | The tendency for initial advantages to compound over time ("the rich get richer") — applied in education to describe how students with stronger starting positions benefit disproportionately from additional resources. |
| MDRC | Originally Manpower Demonstration Research Corporation — a US nonprofit research organisation specialising in rigorous evaluations of social programmes, including education and workforce interventions. |
| Metacognition | Awareness and understanding of one's own thought processes — the ability to monitor, evaluate, and regulate one's learning strategies. A key component of self-regulated learning. |
| Micro-credentials | Short, focused certifications that verify mastery of a specific skill or competency, typically narrower in scope than traditional degrees and designed to be stackable. |
| Minerva University | US-based university (founded 2014) that replaces lectures with small, seminar-style active learning sessions conducted through a proprietary online platform, with students living in different global cities each semester. |
| MOOCs | Massive Open Online Courses — large-scale, open-access online courses offered by platforms such as Coursera, edX, and FutureLearn, typically developed by universities. |
| NLP | Natural Language Processing — the branch of AI concerned with enabling computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language; used in resume parsing and chatbot-based hiring. |
| OfS | Office for Students — the independent regulator of higher education in England (since 2018), responsible for quality assurance, access, and financial sustainability oversight. |
| OIG | Office of Inspector General — US government oversight body; cited in the book for its audit of Western Governors University's compliance with federal financial aid rules. |
| Open Badges | Digital credentials following an open technical standard (maintained by 1EdTech) that encode metadata about the issuer, criteria, and evidence of achievement — verifiable and shareable online. |
| Piscine | French for "swimming pool" — the intensive, multi-week immersive selection and onboarding process used by 42 School, where candidates work on coding challenges under peer assessment. |
| Posse Foundation | US programme that sends cohorts ("posses") of diverse students from urban backgrounds to selective colleges with full scholarships and peer-group support to improve retention and graduation. |
| Prussian system | Historical reference to the standardised, state-run education system developed in 18th/19th-century Prussia, characterised by compulsory attendance, age-based cohorts, and uniform curricula — often cited as the template for modern mass schooling. |
| Quintuple Helix | Innovation model involving five sectors — academia, industry, government, civil society, and the natural environment — as co-creators of knowledge and policy. |
| RAPID framework | Decision-making framework that assigns clear roles: Recommend, Agree, Perform, Input, Decide — used in the book to analyse stakeholder responsibilities in education reform. |
| RCT | Randomised Controlled Trial — an experimental design where participants are randomly assigned to treatment and control groups; considered the gold standard for causal evidence. |
| Russell Group | Association of 24 leading UK research-intensive universities (including Oxford, Cambridge, and Imperial College London) that collectively receive a disproportionate share of research funding. |
| SHRM | Society for Human Resource Management — the world's largest HR professional association; cited in the book for employer survey data on hiring practices, skills-based hiring adoption, and micro-credential recognition. |
| Scaffolded progression | Instructional design approach where support structures are gradually reduced as learners develop competence and independence — based on Vygotsky's zone of proximal development. |
| Self-determination theory | Psychological theory (Deci & Ryan) proposing that motivation and well-being depend on three basic needs: autonomy, competence, and relatedness — influential in educational design. |
| Self-regulated learning | The capacity to set goals, monitor progress, select strategies, and adjust behaviour during learning — a key predictor of academic success and a core component of learner agency. |
| Signalling stack | Conceptual framework used in the book describing four layers of credential signalling: (1) institutional credentials (degrees), (2) modular credentials (micro-credentials, certificates), (3) portfolio evidence (projects, code, publications), and (4) behavioural signals (learning velocity, collaboration patterns). |
| Signalling theory | Economic theory (Spence, 1973) explaining how credentials function as signals of unobservable qualities (ability, persistence) to employers in labour markets with information asymmetry. |
| Skills-based hiring | Recruitment approach that evaluates candidates on demonstrated competencies and task performance rather than formal degrees or institutional pedigree. |
| SkillsFuture | Singapore's national initiative providing citizens with credits and frameworks for lifelong learning, integrating schools, employers, and credentialing bodies around portable qualifications. |
| SME | Small and Medium-sized Enterprise — businesses below defined thresholds of employees and turnover; in the book, discussed in relation to their disadvantage in adopting AI hiring tools and structured training. |
| SNHU | Southern New Hampshire University — a US institution that has grown into one of the largest online universities, often cited as an example of scaling competency-based and online education. |
| Source epistemology | The capacity to evaluate not just whether a claim is true, but the credibility of its source, the incentive structures behind it, and the alternative sources that may exist — a form of media literacy for the algorithmic age. |
| STEM | Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics — the cluster of academic disciplines frequently prioritised in education and workforce policy. |
| Stratification trap | Pattern where educational innovations that promise equity instead reinforce existing inequalities, because advantaged learners are better positioned to exploit new opportunities. |
| Talent marketplace | Internal platform (e.g. Gloat, Fuel50, Eightfold.ai) that uses AI to match employees with projects, roles, mentoring, and learning opportunities within their organisation. |
| Teaming and Twinning | Mentoring and collaboration model pairing institutions or individuals across resource levels to enable knowledge transfer and mutual development — used in the book as an equity mechanism. |
| TestGorilla | Pre-employment assessment platform offering skills-based testing as an alternative to CV screening, covering cognitive ability, technical skills, and personality. |
| Tier 1–4 evidence | The book's evidence hierarchy: Tier 1 = meta-analyses and large-scale RCTs; Tier 2 = quasi-experimental and multi-site studies; Tier 3 = single-institution pilots and case studies; Tier 4 = vendor-reported data and marketing claims. |
| Transfer (learning) | The ability to apply knowledge and skills acquired in one context to new, different situations — a central goal of education and a persistent challenge in instructional design. |
| Transatlantic regulatory gap | The divergence between the EU's prescriptive approach to AI regulation (EU AI Act) and the US's lighter-touch, sector-specific approach, creating different operating conditions for AI in hiring and education. |
| TrueProfile.io | Digital platform for verifying and sharing professional and educational credentials, particularly used in healthcare and regulated professions. |
| Unbundling | The disaggregation of functions traditionally combined within a single institution (e.g. content delivery, assessment, credentialing, socialisation, research at a university) into separate services provided by different actors. |
| UNESCO | United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization — the UN agency responsible for international cooperation in education, science, culture, and communication. |
| UNICEF | United Nations Children's Fund — the UN agency focused on children's rights, health, and education; cited for data on learning poverty and youth outcomes. |
| USAID | United States Agency for International Development — the US government agency responsible for civilian foreign aid and development, including education programmes. |
| VR/AR | Virtual Reality / Augmented Reality — immersive technologies used in education and hiring for simulations, skills assessments, and experiential learning. |
| WEF | World Economic Forum — international organisation known for the annual Davos meeting; frequently cited for reports on future of work, skills, and education. |
| WGU | Western Governors University — a US nonprofit online university offering competency-based degree programmes, where students advance by passing assessments rather than completing courses on a fixed schedule. |
| Wissenschaftliche Mitarbeiter | Academic staff in the German university system — typically early-career researchers on temporary contracts who combine research, teaching, and administrative duties. Their precarious employment conditions are a recurring structural issue. |
| Year Up | US workforce development programme targeting young adults from under-resourced backgrounds, combining technical training with corporate internships and wraparound support. |
| Zone of proximal development | Concept (Vygotsky) describing the gap between what a learner can do independently and what they can achieve with appropriate guidance — the optimal zone for instructional intervention. |
