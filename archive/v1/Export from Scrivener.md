# Introduction #


Education is Broken
Future Frontiers in Higher Education and Workforce Evolution  
If you are picking up this book, you have probably felt it already: something about education no longer fits the world we live in. A degree costs more than a car, new skills go out of date in a few years, and many students and workers feel anxious, burnt out, or simply lost.[1]
At the same time, extraordinary new possibilities have appeared. A teenager in a small town can learn advanced math from an AI tutor at midnight. A nurse can take an online micro-course and qualify for new responsibilities at work. A 40-year-old programmer can retrain for a new role using employer-funded learning, guided by recommendation systems that feel closer to Netflix than to school.[1]
So is education broken—or is it being rebuilt? The honest answer is: both. The old structures are under strain, but the new ones are uneven, confusing, and sometimes unfair.[1]
This book is my attempt to make sense of that transition in plain language.[1]

This is not a book about how to “hack” your grades, find the perfect university, or pick the one right career. It is also not a technical guide to artificial intelligence, nor a nostalgic defence of the past.[1]
Instead, it is a field guide to a landscape that is changing under our feet. We will look at three places where education now happens:[1]

* Universities and colleges.
* The workplace and corporate learning.
* Hiring and recruiting, where credentials are tested against real jobs.[1]

In each place, we will ask three simple questions:

1. What used to work here, and why?
2. What is breaking, and for whom?
3. What is being tried instead—and what could possibly go wrong?[1]

You do not need any background in education research or technology to follow the argument. Whenever we talk about a new model or tool, we will anchor it in real examples and everyday decisions: whether to enroll in a degree, take a short course, trust an AI tutor, or believe a new credential.[1]

The book is in three parts, but you can also dip in and out.

* Part I looks at higher education. We meet the “intelligence factory”—a metaphor for AI systems that can teach and test at scale—and compare it to the traditional university that combines lectures, campus life, and degrees. We explore new models: competency-based online universities, global campus-free institutions, peer-driven coding schools, European university alliances, and micro-credentials. We also take a hard look at the pitfalls: credential inflation, algorithmic bias, stratification between elite and non-elite institutions, and the risk that exciting experiments mainly benefit already-privileged learners.[1]
* Part II moves into companies. Here we see how employers are turning learning into a continuous process: AI systems recommend training, track skill development, and suggest internal moves. Programs like employer-sponsored tuition platforms promise to give millions of workers a “second chance” to gain qualifications without debt. Again, there are trade-offs: when your employer finances your learning, who decides what you study and how your data is used?[1]
* Part III examines recruiting. We look at AI tools that scan CVs, analyze portfolios, run simulations, and even try to read emotions in interviews. We explore skills-based hiring, where what you can do starts to matter more than where you studied. We also see how uneven and messy this shift is, and why degrees still matter a great deal in many sectors.[1]

Across all three parts, one theme keeps returning: signals. A degree, a micro-credential, a portfolio, a project, a performance review, a simulation score—all are ways to signal ability in a noisy world. AI does not remove the need for signals; it multiplies them. That can be liberating, but it can also be overwhelming.[1]

I wrote this book for people who feel caught between the old and new worlds of learning.[1]

* If you are a student or parent, you will find context for decisions about degrees, alternative programs, and how much to trust AI tools.[1]
* If you teach or work in a university, you will see how your daily struggles with funding, curriculum, and technology fit into a bigger picture.[1]
* If you are a manager or HR professional, you will find a roadmap for linking internal learning, external programs, and hiring practices without losing sight of people.[1]
* If you are simply a curious citizen, you will see why the way we organize learning is one of the most important political and economic questions of this decade.[1]

I assume you are busy. Each chapter is written to stand on its own, with summaries and concrete examples. You can read the book straight through, or skip to the topics that matter most to you.[1]

By the end of the book, I hope you will be able to:

* See clearly which parts of education are truly broken, and which are still valuable but under pressure.[1]
* Recognize the main new models and their strengths and weaknesses, without getting lost in buzzwords.[1]
* Understand how universities, companies, and hiring systems are linked, so changes in one place do not surprise you in another.[1]
* Ask better questions of any new program, platform, or policy you encounter: Who benefits? Who is excluded? What is being measured—and what is not?[1]

Most of all, I hope you come away with a sense that the future of education is not fixed by technology alone. It will be shaped by choices—by institutions, employers, governments, and by people like you deciding how and where to learn.[1]
This book is an invitation to take those choices seriously.[1]
