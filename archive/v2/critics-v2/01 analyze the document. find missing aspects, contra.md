Here is a first-pass critique focused on structure, logic, and argumentative completeness rather than content quality.

## 1. Global thesis and scope

- The main claim “education is broken” is elaborated into four dimensions (learning outcomes, equity, cost, labour-market fit), then extended into AI, hiring, corporate learning, stratification, European governance, etc.[^1]
Missing step: The book never fully resolves whether this is a claim about “higher education in OECD countries,” “global education,” or “higher and corporate learning in AI-rich economies.” The scope shifts across chapters without a clear meta-level clarification of what counts as in‑scope versus illustrative.[^1]
- The rhetoric oscillates between “this is a systemic breakdown” and “this is a hybrid transition with positive potential,” but there is no explicit reconciliation of these tones into a single, testable thesis.[^1]

Suggestion: Add an explicit “What exactly is the thesis of this book?” section at the end of chapter 1 that pins down:

- Geography (primarily OECD / Europe + US?)
- Level (tertiary + corporate learning + hiring)
- Normative claim (broken beyond repair vs transformable under which conditions).


## 2. Evidence use vs normative claims

- The text carefully annotates sources and even provides an “evidence hierarchy,” which creates expectations of strong empirical backing.[^1]
Missing step: Several of the strongest normative claims (e.g., “the future belongs to those who…”; or categorical statements about AI-era skills or portfolio dominance in hiring) rest on consultancy reports, vendor case studies, or single-company examples that are explicitly marked as Tier M or Tier O.[^1]
- There is a tension between the careful methodological note and the frequent extrapolation from weak evidence to broad predictions (time horizons for degree erosion, agency divides, AI skills wage premia, effectiveness of “learning in the flow of work”).[^1]

Suggestion: Each chapter should have a short “evidence vs conjecture” box that:

- Labels certain arguments as scenario or hypothesis, not findings.
- Clarifies which claims would reasonably change if better data contradicted them.


## 3. Causal chains that are implied but not shown

### 3.1 From AI capabilities to institutional unbundling

- The “intelligence factory” chapter presents a clean unbundling story (content, networks, credential) and claims AI makes each component independently available and often superior.[^1]
Missing step: The causal chain from “technical capability exists” to “students/employers actually defect from bundled universities at scale” is not spelled out. We see mechanisms (AI tutors, adaptive learning, portfolios) but not how they overcome institutional inertia, regulatory barriers, status games, and signalling equilibria.[^1]

Needed bridge elements:

- Explicit discussion of network effects and prestige inertia in elite institutions.
- Conditions under which employers would genuinely stop using degrees as cheap filters (e.g., regulation, liability, HR incentives).


### 3.2 From skills-based rhetoric to actual hiring practice

- The text itself notes that employers talk skills-based hiring but still rely on degrees as filters.[^1]
Contradiction / tension: Later, the book assumes a fairly strong shift toward portfolio/skills-first labour markets in 5–20 years and uses that to motivate the “signalling stack” argument.[^1]
Missing step: A formal labour-market model (even conceptual) that explains under what institutional and cost conditions employers rationally move from degree-based to portfolio-based screening, and what could stall this shift indefinitely.


## 4. Internal tensions and unaddressed counter-cases

### 4.1 Elite vs mass systems

- The critique of Minerva, 42, and CBE is that they are structurally selective and often repackage privilege.[^1]
Tension: The “intelligence factory” and hybrid university vision implicitly assume exactly the sort of highly self-directed, AI-literate, project-capable learner that these models already target.[^1]
Missing step: An explicit treatment of how the proposed hybrid system would avoid reproducing the same elite bias, given that its success depends on high agency, digital fluency, and self-regulation.


### 4.2 Universities as “crucibles” vs cost crisis

- The book argues that universities must double down on research, character formation, civic mission, and high-trust environments, not content delivery.[^1]
Tension: These “crucible” functions are labour- and capital-intensive, while the cost chapter presents universities as already financially squeezed and underfunded.[^1]
Missing step: A more concrete economic model showing how institutions can finance a pivot away from mass lectures toward intensive crucible experiences without exacerbating cost and access problems.

For example:

- What happens to class sizes, faculty–student ratios, and per-student cost?
- Which trade-offs are acceptable (e.g., fewer programmes, more selective intake, more taxation, differential pricing)?


## 5. System boundaries and feedback loops

- The book is strong on listing actors (governments, accreditors, employers, EdTech, students) and frictions.[^1]
Missing step: A consistent system model across chapters (e.g., stock–flow or feedback framework) that ties together:
    - How hiring practices reshape education supply.
    - How corporate learning interacts with public universities.
    - How European governance and demographic trends feed back into institutional strategies.[^1]

Right now, feedback loops are described in narrative fragments; they are not pulled together into a single synthetic model that could be used for scenario thinking.

## 6. Conceptual gaps

### 6.1 Pedagogy and learning science

- The book speaks extensively about structures (governance, funding, credentials, AI infrastructure) but comparatively less about concrete pedagogy and learning science beyond competency-based education, adaptive tools, and active learning.[^1]
Missing aspect: A more systematic integration of what we know about motivation, cognitive load, transfer, metacognition, and socio-emotional learning into the proposed new models.

Questions that remain open:

- How exactly do “crucibles” foster judgment and character in ways that avoid elitist, hazing-like or exclusionary dynamics?
- How do we scaffold agency and self-regulation for students who do not start as “self-directed learners”?


### 6.2 Non-work purposes of education

- The book repeatedly affirms universities’ civic, cultural, and identity functions, but most detailed argumentation returns to labour-market fit, AI skills, and employer expectations.[^1]
Missing aspect: A more developed argument on non-economic purposes, with concrete implications:
    - What would a curriculum look like that genuinely foregrounds civic education in AI-era conditions of information abundance and polarization?
    - How should we measure success in those domains, given the critique of narrow metrics?


## 7. Equity logic: strong diagnosis, weaker operationalisation

- The stratification chapter is one of the strongest, especially on the agency divide and the risk of models that “work beautifully for people who don’t actually need institutions.”[^1]
Missing step: When the book later prescribes design anchors (equity as design constraint, digital baselines, mental-health infrastructure, disaggregated outcomes), it largely stays at principle level.[^1]

Gaps:

- No explicit discussion of who pays for these equity constraints in systems already struggling with funding.
- Limited engagement with politically difficult trade-offs (e.g., redistributive funding formulas, caps on high-fee international recruitment, regulation of predatory providers).
- Little on teacher capacity-building and working conditions as equity levers, despite an earlier acknowledgement of burnout and low pay.[^1]


## 8. Temporal consistency and scenario work

- A scenario matrix is announced (“A Scenario Matrix: What Shapes the Next Decade”) and “Three Futures for Education” is outlined at the end.[^1]
Missing step: The scenarios are not consistently used as scaffolding for earlier chapters. Instead, many predictions slip back into a single, implicit “most likely” future (hybrid, skills-first, portfolio-emphasising).[^1]

Suggestion:

- Make scenarios an explicit analytical backbone: each major claim (about AI in teaching, skills-based hiring, corporate learning dominance, European governance outcomes) should be tagged as primarily applying to one scenario (Drift, Platform World, Redesign) and treated as conditional, not universal.


## 9. Localised contradictions or frictions

A few smaller but notable tensions:

- Standardised testing:
    - Early chapters emphasise that standardised tests are narrow and often flawed.[^1]
    - Later, AI-driven analytics and micro-credentials rely heavily on measurable, standardised performance for scale and trust.[^1]
Missing step: A clearer reconciliation of the critique of traditional testing with the reliance on new forms of scalable measurement; what quality criteria distinguish “good” AI-era assessments from the old ones?
- Self-directed learners:
    - “Self-directed learning” is criticised as often code for “no institutional support,” systematically favouring the already privileged.[^1]
    - At the same time, the book positions high agency and learning velocity as crucial AI-era meta-skills, without fully specifying how institutions can reliably teach these beyond high-level calls for coaching and scaffolding.[^1]
- Corporate learning as second chance:
    - Corporate learning is framed both as compensating for university failures and as structurally constrained by employer priorities and curriculum narrowing.[^1]
Missing step: An explicit typology of when corporate learning strengthens public goals (e.g., basic skills, second-chance credentials) and when it undermines them (e.g., overly narrow pipelines, data capture, limited portability).
